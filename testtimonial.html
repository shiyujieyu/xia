More than a hundred years ago, people always thought that there were only six planets in the solar system: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter and Saturn. It was not until March 13, 1781 that people found a new member of the solar system from the astronomical telescope. This is Uranus. After Uranus was discovered, people observed its orbit and found that this planet did not follow a definite orbit when it revolved around the sun, but deviated from its orbit. It is assumed that another planet outside Uranus is interfering with his revolution. On September 23, 1846, the German astronomer Caleb observed a new planet, which is Neptune. However, the matter is not over yet. Scientists have observed that Neptune's trajectory is irregular, and they speculate that there is a planet outside of Neptune. In March 1930, an astronomer named Tom Bo observed a new planet, which was Pluto (later according to the new definition, Pluto was classified as a dwarf planet).

Uranus

Uranus (astronomical symbol: ⛢), one of the eight planets in the solar system, is the seventh planet in the solar system from the inside out (18.37-20.08 astronomical units). Its volume ranks third in the solar system, its mass ranks fourth, and it is almost horizontal. Orbiting around the sun. Uranus is the first planet discovered using a telescope. The internal and atmospheric composition of Uranus and Neptune is different from that of larger gas giants, and astronomers have established classifications of ice giants to define them. Among the eight planets, only the English name of Uranus, Uranus, is derived from Greek mythology rather than Roman mythology. Uranus, the god of the sky in Greek mythology, is the father of Cronus, the god of agriculture, and the grandfather of Zeus, the king of the gods. Herschel, the discoverer of Uranus, once named it "Georgium Sidus", which is now also used by some scholars.

Neptune

Neptune (astronomical symbol: ♆) is one of the eight planets in the solar system, and it is also the planet farthest from the sun in the known solar system. The semi-major axis of Neptune's orbit is 30.07 astronomical units, the revolution period is 164.8 years, the mass is 17.147 earth mass, and the radius is 3.86 earth radius. The highest magnitude of Neptune is about 7.67, and it can be observed only with the help of an astronomical telescope. Neptune is blue to the naked eye, and Westerners named it after the sea god Neptune in Roman mythology. When Li Shanlan and others translated "Talking the Sky" in 1859, they named the Chinese translation Neptune. The chemical composition of Neptune’s atmosphere is dominated by hydrogen molecules and helium. In addition, there are traces of methane in the atmosphere of Neptune, which is one of the reasons that make the planet blue. Neptune's mass is slightly greater than Uranus, its density is slightly greater than Uranus, and its radius is slightly smaller than Uranus.

Pluto

Pluto (the astronomical symbol is ♇) is a dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt. Pluto is the first Kuiper belt object to be discovered, the first pluto-like object, and the largest known dwarf planet in the solar system with the second largest mass. Pluto is the largest outer Neptune celestial body in volume. Among the celestial bodies that directly orbit the sun, Pluto ranks 9th in volume and 10th in mass. Pluto is mainly composed of rocks and ice, and its mass is relatively small, only 1/6 of the mass of the moon and 1/3 of the volume of the moon. In 1930, Clyde Tombaugh discovered Pluto and regarded it as the ninth largest planet. . However, due to the small mass of Pluto, the International Astronomical Union formally defined the concept of planets in 2006 and excluded Pluto from the ranks of planets and reclassified it as a dwarf planet. Pluto was first proposed by Venetia Burney, a female student of Oxford University, to name this planet after Pluto, the god of the underworld in Roman mythology.

The ancients collectively called the sun, the moon, and the five planets (gold, wood, water, fire, and earth) Qi Yao. Babylon once used seven lunar days to record the sun, in the order of sun, moon, fire, water, wood, gold, and earth, from Sunday to Saturday, so it is also called "week". It was introduced to China in the 8th century. The concept of the calendar with Qi Yao as the week was introduced into China from India, and the concept of the week with Europe seems to have originated from the Mesopotamia Ancient Babylonian civilization, which is the same lunar calendar.
