star

Stars are giant spheres made of luminous plasma, mainly hydrogen, helium, and traces of heavier elements. On a fine night, there are always countless spots of light embedded in the night. Except for a few planets, most of them are stars. The sun is the closest star to the earth, and the stars that can be seen at night are almost all in the Milky Way. Of the approximately 300 billion stars in the Milky Way system, humans can only observe a small portion. The star will undergo nuclear fusion in the core to generate energy and transmit it outward, and then radiate from the surface to outer space. Once the nuclear reaction in the core is exhausted, the star's life is about to end. At the end of life, stars will also contain degenerate matter. The difference in star size and mass will lead to different endings: white dwarf stars, neutron stars, black holes.

white dwarf

A white dwarf is a star with low luminosity, high density and high temperature. Because of its white color and small size, it was named a white dwarf. With a surface temperature of 8000K, it emits white light and has a life span of several billion years.

Neutron star

Neutron stars are the densest stellar bodies other than black holes. Stars evolve to the end. After a supernova explosion occurs through gravitational collapse, they may become one of the few end points. The mass of a star that does not reach the black hole can be formed at the end of its life. A star between a white dwarf and a black hole.

black hole

A black hole is a kind of celestial body that exists in the universe in modern general relativity. The gravitational force of a black hole is extremely powerful, making the escape velocity in the horizon greater than the speed of light. Therefore, "a black hole is a celestial body whose space-time curvature is so great that light cannot escape from its event horizon."

Humans have a long history of observing stars, and there are many ways to observe them. The brighter stars are divided into individual constellations and constellations, and some stars have their own names. The brightness of a star is called its magnitude, and the brighter the star, the lower its magnitude. Astronomers have also compiled star catalogs to facilitate research.
